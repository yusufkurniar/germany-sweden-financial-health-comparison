The COVID-19 pandemic caused significant disruptions across global markets, testing the financial resilience of firms in every sector. In Germany, the first confirmed case was recorded on 27 January 2020. Nationwide contact restrictions began on 22 March 2020. Most nationwide measures were lifted between 20 March and 2 April 2022, and the final federal provisions expired on 7 April 2023.

This project examines how German companies responded to these shocks during 2020 – 2021, using financial data from the Bureau van Dijk OSIRIS Database. A reliable global source of harmonised corporate information. The dataset available for this study covers 2018 - 2021. The period from 2018–2019 provides the pre-pandemic baseline, while 2020 – 2021 captures the pandemic period. Data for 2022 are not available in this extract.

Our analysis focuses on how industries differed in their financial outcomes during this period, investigating changes in profitability, leverage, liquidity, and firm size. By identifying which sectors showed unexpected resilience or vulnerability, we aim to understand how structural and strategic differences shaped recovery from the pandemic’s economic impacts. All analytical procedures follow the ETC5521 workflow, ensuring reproducibility, transparency, and alignment with the course framework for exploratory data analysis.

The dataset used in this analysis originates from the Bureau van Dijk OSIRIS database, a comprehensive source of harmonised financial information for publicly listed and major private firms worldwide. For this study, we extracted firm-level data for German companies over the 2018–2021 accounting years, a period that captures both pre-pandemic and pandemic-era financial performance. Each record represents a firm’s reported financials for a specific accounting year, enabling longitudinal exploration of trends in profitability, liquidity, leverage, and resilience. #build a single data frame with a 'year' column taken from the filename
combined_germany <- {
  germany_parts <- vector("list", length(osiris_germany))
  names(germany_parts) <- names(osiris_germany)

  for (file_stem in names(osiris_germany)) {
    data_frame_from_file <- osiris_germany[[file_stem]]
    extracted_year <- as.integer(str_extract(file_stem, "\\d{4}"))

    germany_parts[[file_stem]] <- mutate(data_frame_from_file, year = extracted_year)
  }

  #bind and sort by year (optional)
  bind_rows(germany_parts) |>
    arrange(year)
}

The selected variables encompass four key dimensions of corporate financial health:

  • Identification and Location - company_id, company_name, country, and city provide firm-level identifiers and geographical context.
  • Balance Sheet Structure - total_assets_eur, total_liabilities_eur, and total_equity_eur measure firm size and capital composition, reported in euros.
  • Income and Profitability - total_revenue_eur, net_sales_eur, and net_income_eur capture income statement outcomes, while ebit_margin_pct, ebitda_margin_pct, roa_pct, and roe_pct reflect operating performance and returns to shareholders.

  • Solvency and Liquidity - solvency_pct, gearing_pct, current_ratio, liquidity_ratio, shareholders_liq_pct, and interest_cover assess each firm’s financial stability, leverage, and capacity to meet short-term obligations.

  • Industry Classification - industry_nace, industry_icb, and industry_group link firms to their respective economic sectors according to European (NACE) and global (ICB) coding systems.

It is important to note that the accounting year variable in OSIRIS reflects two-year reporting periods. For example, the 2018 accounting year represents financial results spanning 2017–2018, 2019 corresponds to 2018–2019, 2020 covers 2019–2020, and 2021 includes 2020–2021. Throughout this report, each accounting year is interpreted as representing the endpoint of its reporting cycle. Consequently, “2021” is treated as capturing firm performance during the height of the COVID-19 pandemic, while 2018–2019 represent pre-pandemic benchmarks.

This dataset structure enables a robust exploration of temporal changes in corporate performance and the role of firm characteristics , particularly firm size , in shaping financial resilience throughout the pandemic period.

Research Question

Here are the research questions (sub-questions) that guide us in analysing and answering the primary question: How did the pandemic affect corporate financials in Germany?

1. How did profitability change across industries?

This question assesses how profitability variables, such as ROA, EBITDA, or margins shifted across industries over time, highlighting which sectors improved, remained steady, or declined during the pre-pandemic period (2018 – 2019) and the pandemic (2020 – 2021) in Germany. These cross-industry differences reveal relative exposure, resilience, and recovery pathways during the pandemic compared to the pre-pandemic period. Understanding the direction and scale of profitability changes directly addresses the main question of how the pandemic affected corporate finances.

2. How did leverage (debt-to-assets) and liquidity (current ratio) evolve during pandemic?

This question examines how German firms managed their financial structure and short-term liquidity during the COVID-19 period. By tracking leverage and liquidity trends, we can assess whether firms relied more on debt financing or preserved liquidity to withstand operational shocks. Understanding these shifts helps explain how the pandemic influenced firms’ balance sheet resilience and capacity to absorb financial stress.

3. Which German industries showed unexpected financial resilience or vulnerability from 2020 to 2022, and how were these outcomes shaped by profitability, leverage, liquidity, and firm size?

This research question seeks to examine how key financial indicators—profitability, leverage, liquidity, and firm size—contributed to the varying levels of resilience or vulnerability observed across German industries during the pandemic period. By analysing these factors, the study aims to understand the financial dynamics that enabled certain sectors to withstand economic shocks while others experienced prolonged instability. The findings can provide insights into industry-specific financial strengths and weaknesses, offering implications for future crisis preparedness and policy design.

4. How did the fundamental relationship between corporate liquidity and profitability evolve and fracture within industries during the pandemic?

The COVID-19 pandemic (2020–2022) placed severe pressure on global corporate financial structures. Firms faced the challenge of maintaining short-term liquidity without compromising profitability. Financial theory suggests that adequate liquidity supports stable profits by ensuring operational continuity. However, this relationship may have fractured across industries amid uneven shocks and policy responses. This question examines how liquidity (Current Ratio) and profitability (ROA) evolved among German firms during the pandemic. It helps reveal whether the pandemic reshaped the traditional balance between liquidity and profitability in corporate financial resilience.

normalize_icb <- function(x){
  x_chr <- str_extract(as.character(x), "\\d+")

Threshold-based industry classification

Given the exploratory focus of our study and limited emphasis on clustering methods in lectures, we instead used threshold-based segmentation to classify industries into recovery archetypes. This approach categorizes industries based on their ROA trend strength and volatility using the following criteria:

  • High trend strength (≥0.7) + Low spikiness (≤median) → “Resilient industries”

  • Low trend strength (≤0.3) + High spikiness (≥75th percentile) → “K-shaped recovery industries”

  • High trend strength (≥0.7) + High spikiness (≥75th percentile) → “Hidden crisis industries”

  • All other combinations → “Moderate impact industries”

Based on criteria, the analysis reveals clear patterns of differential recovery across sectors. The Construction and Financial & Insurance sectors demonstrate resilient characteristics, maintaining stable performance throughout the pandemic period.   1. Micro-level evidence of anomalous recovery patterns across industries (ROA and liquidity) - scagnostics (Tignostics) (Lecture 8, 22-27 & Lecture 9, slide 24)

In this section we track how the firm-level relationship between liquidity (Current Ratio) and profitability (ROA) changed during the COVID-19 period. We approach is to treat each firm as a point in the CR–ROA plane, then study the shape of each industry’s point cloud year by year. We use scagnostic metrics to quantify those shapes, and we fit a simple linear model (ROA ~ Current Ratio) for every industry × year to extract slope and residual volatility. We treat 2020 as the COVID year and compare values before and after 2020.

Data and methods

We select industries previously identified in the macro analysis (industries labelled K-shaped, Resilient, or Hidden Crisis). For each industry × fiscal year we:

  • keep firm-level pairs (current_ratio, roa_pct);

  • calculate scagnostic metrics: outlying, stringy, striated, clumpy, sparse, monotonic, dcor;

  • fit a linear model roa_pct ~ current_ratio and record slope and residual SD;

  • compute year-to-year slope changes and count “jumps” (a jump = |slope change| > 0.5);

  • compute pre/post differences in mean residual SD and mean slope (post − pre).

  • When an industry × year has too few firms (< 3) the scagnostics or regression are not reported (NA).

How to read the scagnostic metrics

  • Clumpy: higher values indicate the points form separate clusters → suggests within-industry divergence (possible K-shaped pattern).

  • Outlying: higher values indicate presence of extreme firms (very strong survivors or very weak firms).

  • Stringy / Monotonic: higher values indicate a clearer linear or monotonic relationship between CR and ROA.

  • Residual SD: higher residual SD from the linear fit signals more unexplained scatter (higher micro volatility).

by_ind_year <- by_ind_year |>
  mutate(
    scags = map(data, ~ {
      df <- .x
      # When there are too few samples, return a row of tibble with the same column names and types.
      if (nrow(df) < 3) {
        return(as_tibble(set_names(as.list(rep(NA_real_, length(scag_names))), scag_names)))
      }
      res <- calc_scags(df$current_ratio, df$roa_pct, scags = scag_names)
      # Make sure to convert to atomic vectors and name them, then convert to tibble
      res_vec <- unlist(res)
      res_named <- set_names(as.numeric(res_vec[scag_names]), scag_names)
      as_tibble(as.list(res_named))
    })
  ) |>
  unnest(cols = scags)  # Expand into multiple columns

# Fit a linear regression for each industry × year and extract slope, intercept, and resid_sd
by_ind_year <- by_ind_year |>
  mutate(
    fit = map(data, ~ {
      df <- .x
      if (nrow(df) < 3 || all(is.na(df$current_ratio)) || all(is.na(df$roa_pct))) return(NULL)
      lm(roa_pct ~ current_ratio, data = df)
    }),
    slope = map_dbl(fit, ~ if (is.null(.x)) NA_real_ else coef(.x)[["current_ratio"]]),
    intercept = map_dbl(fit, ~ if (is.null(.x)) NA_real_ else coef(.x)[["(Intercept)"]]),
    resid_sd = map_dbl(fit, ~ if (is.null(.x)) NA_real_ else sd(residuals(.x), na.rm = TRUE))
  ) |>
  select(-fit)

# Take industry as a time series and calculate its evolution characteristics
industry_tignostics <- by_ind_year |>
  arrange(industry_group, fy_year) |>
  group_by(industry_group) |>
  mutate(
    slope_lag = dplyr::lag(slope),
    slope_change = slope - slope_lag,   # year-to-year change in slope
    slope_pct_change = (slope - slope_lag) / abs(slope_lag) * 100,
    resid_sd_lag = dplyr::lag(resid_sd),
    resid_sd_change = resid_sd - resid_sd_lag,
    # jumps: count of year-to-year slope changes bigger than a threshold (e.g. 0.5)
    big_slope_jump = if_else(abs(slope_change) > 0.5, 1, 0),
    jumps_cumulative = cumsum(replace_na(big_slope_jump, 0))
  ) |>
  ungroup()

# Calculate the overall volatility_shift (post - pre) before and after the epidemic (taking 2020 as the epidemic year)

    fy_year < 2020 ~ "pre",
    fy_year == 2020 ~ "covid_year",
    fy_year > 2020 ~ "post"

  group_by(industry_group, period) |>

  1. Summary and interpretation

The exploratory analysis demonstrates that the relationship between corporate profitability and liquidity evolved unevenly across industries during the pandemic. More importantly, the recovery patterns were not the same across or within industries, showing clear evidence of a K-shaped recovery in some sectors.

At the macro level, trend strength and volatility analysis revealed clear segmentation: some industries such as Construction and Finance showed resilient recovery, while others, like Accommodation, Information and Recreation, exhibited K-shaped patterns with disrupted ROA trends and high volatility.

The micro-level scagnostic analysis further confirmed this divergence. After removing several industries which have small yearly samples (Construction,
Accommodation & Food, and Administrative & Support), the industries showing K-shaped tendencies at the macro level exhibited notable shifts in scagnostic measures. Specifically, Financial & Insurance displayed a clear rise in clumpiness, signaling within-sector fragmentation, while Arts, Entertainment & Recreation showed growing outlying behavior, reflecting the emergence of extreme performers. In contrast, resilient sectors maintained relatively stable and monotonic liquidity–profitability relationships throughout the period.

These results suggest that the pandemic not only altered the overall financial dynamics between liquidity and profitability but also intensified disparities within certain industries, forming a distinct K-shaped recovery pattern.

According to Q1, median profitability remained broadly stable between 2018 – 2019 and 2020 – 2021, but dispersion increased. The interesting result is that the Accommodation and Food sectors improved despite lockdowns, while the Health Care sector declined sharply, and ROA and ROE exhibited heavier lower tails. Additionally, for Q2, balance sheets remained steady as liquidity changed little and leverage increased only slightly. The key point is that firms did not leverage up even when credit was cheap.

According to Q3 and Q4, resilience correlates with stronger liquidity and moderate leverage, with the Health Care, Technology, and Utilities sectors showing more resilience, while Hospitality, Retail, and Manufacturing are more vulnerable. The interesting part is that the link between liquidity and profitability weakened as many firms protected cash at the expense of near-term margins.

Overall, the pandemic’s impact on German corporate finances was moderate but uneven: typical profitability was maintained, balance sheets remained steady, and the main changes involved greater dispersion and sectoral divergence. The crisis highlighted existing gaps, strengthening resilient sectors and revealing vulnerabilities in more leveraged, cyclical industries.
